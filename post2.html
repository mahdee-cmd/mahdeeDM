Selecting a Process: 3D Printing the Phone Casing

Printer selection, print settings, failures encountered, and the real cost of additive manufacturing at scale.

Choosing the Right Printer

The first prototype casing was printed on a Prusa MK3S, an open-frame FDM printer widely used in educational and prototyping environments. PLA filament was used across all prints due to its dimensional stability, ease of use, and suitability for functional prototypes. Gibson et al. (2015) note that PLA is one of the most widely used FDM materials due to its low warping tendency and good surface resolution relative to other thermoplastics, making it well suited to parts where fit and assembly are critical.

However, the initial Prusa print revealed two significant problems. The open-frame design of the MK3S offered no protection from ambient temperature fluctuations in the lab environment, which contributed to slight warping at the edges of the casing as it cooled unevenly on the bed. Additionally, the support structures generated for the overhanging features of the casing were poorly formed thick, difficult to remove, and leaving a rough surface finish on the areas they contacted. As a result of these issues, the decision was made to switch to a Bambu Labs P1S for all subsequent prints. The P1S is a high-speed enclosed FDM printer that eliminates the ambient temperature problem through its sealed chamber, and offers significantly better support structure quality through its multi-material and advanced slicing capabilities. The remaining four prints were all completed on the P1S, with measurable improvements in both dimensional accuracy and surface finish.

Problems & Solutions

Print 1 — Prusa MK3S: Warping and Support Issues The first print on the Prusa MK3S encountered two problems immediately. The first was warping, the edges of the casing lifted slightly off the print bed during cooling, causing a dimensional deviation at the base of the part. This is a well-documented issue with open-frame FDM printers, where uncontrolled airflow and ambient temperature variation causes uneven thermal contraction across the part as it cools (Ngo et al., 2018). The second problem was support structure quality. The overhanging features of the phone casing required support material, and the supports generated by the Prusa's slicer were thick and poorly attached to the part, leaving a heavily scarred surface finish on the contact areas when removed. The combination of these two issues made the first print unsuitable for testing on the FESTO assembly line.

Prints 2–5 — Bambu Labs P1S: Refinement and Improvement Switching to the Bambu Labs P1S resolved both of the Prusa's primary issues. The enclosed chamber eliminated ambient temperature variation, producing flat, warp-free parts from the second print onwards. Support quality also improved significantly, with cleaner detachment and less surface scarring. However, two new issues emerged across the Bambu prints. The first was surface finish, at 0.2mm layer height, visible layer lines remained on the outer faces of the casing. While functionally acceptable, this raised questions about aesthetic quality for a consumer product. Light sanding was applied to later prints to improve the finish without affecting print time. The second issue was dimensional accuracy — when tested on the FESTO assembly line, a laser measurement station identified that one area of the casing was just below the minimum specified height on the GA drawing. This was identified on the third print and corrected through a small scaling adjustment in the Bambu slicer software for prints four and five. This iterative approach mirrors the First Article Inspection (FAI) process used in real manufacturing, where the first parts off a new process are comprehensively measured before full production begins.

Cost Analysis

PLA filament costs approximately £20–25 per kilogram across both the Prusa MK3S and Bambu Labs P1S platforms, with Bambu-branded PLA sitting at the higher end at approximately £22–24 per kilogram. Across five prototype prints the total material cost was modest. However, at full production scale of 50,000 units per year across 70 printers, material cost becomes a significant operational expenditure. The Bambu Labs P1S retails at approximately £900–1,000 per unit, meaning the capital cost of 70 machines alone would be in the region of £63,000–£70,000, before installation, maintenance, energy, and the facility space required to house them.

Critical Reflection: Is 3D Printing Right for 50,000 Units/Year?

The printer selection process in this project, starting with the Prusa MK3S and switching to the Bambu Labs P1S after the first print, reflects a real engineering decision-making process: identify a problem, evaluate alternatives, and implement a better solution. The switch resulted in measurable improvements across all subsequent prints, validating the decision. However, the more fundamental question for Phones-R-Us is whether FDM printing is the right process at 50,000 units per year regardless of which printer is used. For prototyping and small-batch production, additive manufacturing offers clear advantages, no tooling costs, rapid design iteration, and the flexibility to make changes between prints at no additional cost, as demonstrated across the five prints in this project. Kalpakjian and Schmid (2014) note that injection moulding, while requiring significant upfront tooling investment of typically £10,000–£100,000 per mould, produces parts in seconds rather than hours and at a substantially lower per-unit cost. At 50,000 units per year, the tooling cost would be amortised relatively quickly and the process would be far more economically efficient than operating 70 FDM printers continuously. That said, 3D printing retains one critical advantage for a modular phone manufacturer: design flexibility. If Phones-R-Us intends to release multiple casing variants or update designs frequently, the ability to change designs between prints with no tooling cost or lead time may justify the higher per-unit cost — particularly in the early stages of product development before volumes scale significantly.
