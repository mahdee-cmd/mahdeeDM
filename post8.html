Reflection · Week 9–10

Lessons Learned: Digital Manufacturing in Practice

A final reflection on 3D printing, metrology, supplier variability, and the transformative value of virtual factory modelling.

Introduction

This final post reflects on the key lessons learned throughout the 6E7V0013 Digital Manufacturing module — from the first failed prototype on the Prusa MK3S to a fully optimised plant simulation producing 52,290 phones per year. The project has demonstrated in practice what Industry 4.0 promises in theory: that digital tools can fundamentally change how manufacturing decisions are made, reducing risk, cost, and time by solving problems virtually before they arise physically. Schwab (2016) argues that the fourth industrial revolution is defined not by any single technology but by the convergence of digital, physical, and biological systems — and this project has been a direct embodiment of that convergence, combining CAD modelling, additive manufacturing, physical assembly line testing, and discrete event simulation into a single integrated workflow.

What Digital Manufacturing Taught Me

The most significant lesson from this project is that digital tools expose problems that physical intuition cannot. When the FESTO assembly line was simulated in isolation, it appeared to have vast spare capacity — 238,000 phones per year on a standard shift. A manufacturer without simulation tools might have invested in the assembly infrastructure and only discovered the true bottleneck — the 3D printing stage — after committing significant capital. The simulation revealed within minutes what would have taken months and tens of thousands of pounds to discover physically: that the printing process, not the assembly line, was the limiting factor in the entire system, and that 70 printers running in parallel were required to meet the 50,000 unit annual target.

Fusion 360 provided a similar advantage at the design stage. The ability to model the snap fit mechanism using a revolve operation, section-analyse it digitally, and validate the 135° chamfer lead-in before printing a single prototype saved significant material and time. Without CAD, the snap fit geometry would have been developed entirely through physical trial and error — a far slower and more wasteful process. Tao et al. (2018) describe this approach as the digital twin paradigm: a virtual replica of a physical system that enables optimisation in the digital world before commitment in the physical one. This project has been a practical demonstration of exactly that principle.

The Challenge of Measurement & Quality

One of the most valuable — and humbling — lessons of this project was how difficult dimensional accuracy is to achieve consistently in additive manufacturing. The first prototype failed at the FESTO Measuring Station, where a laser sensor identified that a critical dimension of the casing was below the minimum specified height on the GA drawing. What followed was an iterative process of CAD adjustments and print setting refinements — including careful tuning of the ironing settings — before the casing finally passed the measurement check and completed the full FESTO run successfully.

This experience directly reinforced the importance of the PFMEA conducted in Post 5, where dimensional deviation at the Measuring Station was identified as the highest-priority failure mode with an RPN of 288. The fact that this failure occurred in practice — and required multiple iterations to resolve — validates the PFMEA as a genuinely predictive tool rather than a theoretical exercise. Ngo et al. (2018) note that dimensional variation is an inherent characteristic of FDM printing, influenced by thermal contraction, filament consistency, and printer calibration — all of which were directly observed in this project across five print iterations on two different machines.

The Multi-Supplier Problem

A key feature of the Phones-R-Us scenario is that multiple suppliers produce casings to the same GA drawing, which are then assembled together on the FESTO line. In practice, when my final casing was tested alongside the master casing from another supplier, the fit was very similar — the snap fit engaged correctly and no significant gaps were visible at the mating edge. This was a pleasing result, but it should not be taken for granted. The successful fit was the product of five iterative prints, careful dimensional measurement, and targeted CAD corrections — not the result of a robust, controlled manufacturing process that could be reliably repeated at scale.

Brettel et al. (2014) argue that horizontal integration across supplier networks is one of the most significant challenges in Industry 4.0 implementation — achieving true interoperability between independent suppliers requires standardisation of both data formats and manufacturing processes. At the scale of 70 printers potentially operated by multiple manufacturers, the dimensional variability observed across even a small number of prints in this project would multiply significantly. A robust supplier quality management system — including First Article Inspection, statistical process control, and tightly specified interface tolerances — would be essential to maintain consistent assembly performance at production volumes.

The Value of Virtual Factory Modelling

The plant simulation was the single most revealing tool used in this project. Three specific insights emerged that would have been impossible to identify without simulation. First, the bottleneck shift — the assembly line bottleneck (Oven Station, 29 seconds) is completely irrelevant to the system's overall capacity once the printing stage is introduced. The true system bottleneck operates at a completely different timescale (hours vs seconds), and only a full end-to-end simulation makes this visible. Second, the 66 printer shortfall — mathematical projection suggested 66 printers would meet the 50,000 target, but the simulation showed an output of only 49,896, falling 104 units short. This discrepancy, caused by shift transition effects and queuing behaviour, demonstrates that simulation is not just a visualisation tool but a genuinely more accurate analytical method than hand calculation. Third, the production buffer value — the final configuration of 70 printers produces 52,290 units, a surplus of 2,290 that provides a meaningful buffer to absorb printer downtime and quality failures without falling below the customer's minimum order. Negahban and Smith (2014) describe exactly this type of capacity buffer analysis as one of the primary use cases for discrete event simulation in manufacturing system design.

Final Recommendation to Phones-R-Us

Based on the full body of evidence from this project, my recommendation to Phones-R-Us is a hybrid manufacturing strategy: use 3D printing for the current development phase and early production volumes, with a planned transition to injection moulding once annual demand is confirmed and stabilised above 30,000–40,000 units per year.

The case for maintaining 3D printing in the short term is strong. The modular phone concept is inherently design-iterative — new casing variants, different colours, and revised form factors can be introduced without any tooling cost or lead time. This flexibility is the core commercial differentiator of the product, and it would be undermined by committing to injection moulding tooling before the design is fully stabilised. The five-print iteration process in this project is a direct example of this advantage: design changes were implemented between prints at no additional cost.

However, the long-term economics strongly favour injection moulding at the volumes required. Kalpakjian and Schmid (2014) note that injection moulding produces parts in seconds rather than hours, at a per-unit material cost a fraction of FDM printing. At 50,000 units per year the capital cost of injection mould tooling — typically £10,000–£100,000 — would be amortised within one to two years of production, after which the per-unit cost would be dramatically lower than operating 70 FDM printers continuously. The simulation in this project effectively makes the business case for this transition: 70 printers at ~£900–1,000 each represents a capital investment of £63,000–£70,000, plus ongoing energy, maintenance, filament, and labour costs — all of which would be substantially reduced by a single injection moulding tool running at equivalent volume.

The recommended transition point of 30,000–40,000 units per year reflects the crossover at which injection moulding tooling amortisation makes the switch economically rational — a decision that should be modelled in Plant Simulation as a future scenario, using the same methodology developed in this project.
