Analysis · Week 5–7

Process Analysis

Process failure analysis, takt time calculation, value stream mapping, and business modelling for the Phones-R-Us production line.

Introduction

Before committing to a manufacturing process or production system, it is essential to analyse and model it using structured engineering tools. These tools allow potential problems to be identified and resolved before they occur in a real factory; a core principle of digital manufacturing and Industry 4.0. This post documents four key process analysis tools applied to the Phones-R-Us production scenario: Takt Time analysis to verify whether the process can meet customer demand, a Value Stream Map to visualise the end-to-end flow, a Process Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (PFMEA) to identify and mitigate risks, and a Business Model Canvas to frame the wider commercial context. Dittmann et al. (2004) describe PFMEA as a formalised analytical method originating in the NASA Apollo Project, used to identify failures during the planning phase before production begins, making it particularly suited to this project where the consequences of dimensional failure at the FESTO line are directly observable.

Takt Time

Takt time is the maximum time allowed to produce one unit in order to meet customer demand. It is calculated by dividing the available production time by the required output. For Phones-R-Us, the target is 50,000 phones per year on a standard 8-hour shift, 5 days per week, over 48 working weeks per year.

The takt time of 138.2 seconds is significantly longer than the FESTO assembly line bottleneck of 29 seconds (Oven Station), confirming the line has ample capacity to meet the 50,000/year target on a single shift. However, the 3D printing stage at 2 hours (7,200 seconds) per casing vastly exceeds the takt time, requiring 70 printers running in parallel to bring the effective print takt time into line with demand. This confirms that takt time analysis must be applied across the entire value stream rather than individual operations in isolation.

Value Stream Map

The Value Stream Map below visualises the complete production flow from PLA filament supply through 3D printing, quality inspection, FESTO assembly, and final output, highlighting where value is added and where waste exists.

The VSM clearly shows that the 3D printing stage dominates the production lead time at 2 hours per casing, while the entire FESTO assembly process adds only 92 seconds of value. The primary source of waste in the system is the wait time between the printing farm and the assembly line; a queue that only disappears when sufficient printers (70) are running to maintain continuous flow into the FESTO line.

Process Failure Mode & Effects Analysis (PFMEA)

The PFMEA below identifies key potential failure modes across the production process, assessing each for Severity (S), Occurrence (O), and Detectability (D) on a 1–10 scale as defined by the AIAG standard used in the university template. The Risk Priority Number (RPN = S × O × D) determines priority for corrective action. Hofig et al. (2019) note that PFMEA is particularly valuable for multi-stage processes where failure in one stage propagates downstream, directly applicable here where a dimensionally inaccurate casing will halt the FESTO line at the Measuring Station.

The two highest RPN items are warping during 3D printing (RPN 288) and multi-supplier dimensional incompatibility (RPN 200). The warping issue was directly observed and resolved in this project through the switch from the open-frame Prusa MK3S to the enclosed Bambu Labs P1S. The multi-supplier compatibility issue was experienced during the FESTO lab session and highlights the importance of tightly controlled tolerances and First Article Inspection across all suppliers.

Business Model Canvas

The Business Model Canvas frames the Phones-R-Us production scenario within a standard commercial framework (Osterwalder and Pigneur, 2010), identifying the key elements of the business model relevant to this manufacturing project.

Business Model Canvas — Phones-R-Us Modular Phone Production

Key Partners

  • PLA filament suppliers
  • 3D printer manufacturers (Bambu Lab, Prusa Research)
  • PCB and electronics component suppliers
  • Casing manufacturers (this project)
  • FESTO assembly line operators

Key Activities

  • 3D printing of top & bottom casings
  • Dimensional inspection & QC
  • FESTO CP line assembly
  • Plant simulation & capacity planning
  • Supplier coordination

Value Propositions

  • Modular phone — customisable by end user
  • Rapid design iteration via additive manufacturing
  • Scalable production to 50,000+ units/year
  • Industry 4.0 digital manufacturing process
  • No tooling cost for design changes

Customer Relationships

  • B2B supply contract
  • Regular quality reporting
  • First Article Inspection sign-off
  • Continuous improvement

Key Resources

  • 70× Bambu P1S printers
  • FESTO CP assembly line
  • Tecnomatix Plant Simulation
  • Fusion 360 CAD
  • Inspection equipment

Channels

  • Direct B2B supply to Phones-R-Us
  • Batch delivery to production schedule
  • Digital documentation & traceability

Cost Structure

  • Capital: 70× Bambu P1S (~£63,000–£70,000)
  • PLA filament (~£22–24/kg per printer)
  • Energy: 70 printers running continuously
  • Labour: operators & quality inspectors
  • Facility space for 70-printer farm
  • Maintenance & printer depreciation
  • Software licences

Revenue Streams

  • Per-unit supply price to Phones-R-Us
  • Volume contract: 50,000+ units/year
  • Premium for rapid design iteration service
  • Revenue from spare production capacity

The Business Model Canvas highlights that the cost structure is heavily weighted toward capital expenditure on 70 printers and ongoing operational costs. At 50,000 units per year the per-unit cost of 3D printed casings is significantly higher than injection moulded equivalents; the key commercial justification is the modular, design-flexible value proposition, which allows Phones-R-Us to iterate on casing designs without tooling cost or lead time. This advantage diminishes as volumes scale, reinforcing the earlier recommendation that injection moulding should be considered beyond a certain volume threshold.
