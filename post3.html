Prototypes · Week 3–4

Iterative Prototyping

Surface finish, alignment, and supports.

First Prototype

The first prototype was printed on the Prusa MK3S using PLA filament. As shown in Figure 1, the surface finish of this print was poor; significant stringing was visible across the top surface, caused by the extruder travelling between features without retracting the filament sufficiently. The open-frame design of the Prusa offered no protection from ambient temperature fluctuations in the lab, which contributed to inconsistent extrusion and the stringing observed. When the top and bottom casings were assembled together, a slight gap was visible along the mating edge, indicating that warping had occurred during the print. The base of the casing had lifted slightly off the bed during cooling, causing a dimensional deviation that prevented a clean flush fit between the two halves. This first prototype was clearly not suitable for testing on the FESTO assembly line and highlighted the need for both a better printer and refined print settings before a functional casing could be produced.

Prototypes 2, 3 & 4

Switching to the Bambu Labs P1S produced an immediate and significant improvement in print quality across prototypes 2, 3, and 4. The enclosed chamber eliminated the warping and stringing issues seen on the Prusa, parts came off the bed flat, with clean edges and no material trails between features. A key setting introduced on these prints was ironing on the top surface, which passes the nozzle back over the top layer at a reduced flow rate to smooth out any surface imperfections. An ironing speed of 50mm/s and a flow rate of 20% was used for prototypes 2&3 and optimised further on prototype 4 with a 20% flow rate. This produced a noticeably smoother top surface finish compared to the first prototype. There were no stringing issues and no bed adhesion problems across any of the three Bambu prints, confirming that the enclosed environment and improved slicer settings of the P1S resolved the primary issues identified on the Prusa.

Supports

The support structures generated for the first Prusa print were extremely difficult to remove. The default support settings produced thick, densely packed supports that had fused partially with the part surface during printing, meaning significant force was required to detach them. Even after removal, fragments of support material remained embedded in the surface, leaving a rough and visually unacceptable finish on the areas they had contacted.

A print was attempted on the Bambu P1S without any support structures to compare against the heavily supported Prusa print. The result was better than the Prusa in terms of surface cleanliness; without supports there was no scarring or residue on the part surface. However, the overhanging features of the casing showed visible layer sagging on the lower surfaces, and the layer lines were clearly visible, indicating that some form of support was still necessary to achieve a clean result on the more complex geometry of the casing.

The optimal solution was found by using tree supports with PETG as an interface material, with a top Z distance of 0mm, meaning the PETG support made direct contact with the PLA part surface with no gap between them. Because PETG and PLA do not bond to each other chemically, the interface peeled away cleanly and effortlessly after printing, leaving a fully smooth surface with no scarring, residue, or visible support marks. This method combined the structural benefit of full support coverage with the clean release properties of a dissimilar material interface, producing the best surface finish of any support configuration tested.

Final Print

The final print brought together every lesson learned across the previous four iterations. The Bambu Labs P1S was used with PLA filament, ironing enabled at 50mm/s and 22% flow for a smooth top surface finish, and tree supports with a PETG interface at 0mm Z distance for clean, effortless support removal. The result was a dimensionally accurate casing with no warping, no stringing, a smooth top surface, and fully clean support contact areas. When assembled with the top casing, the snap fit engaged correctly with no visible gap at the mating edge, confirming that the warping issues from the first prototype had been fully resolved. This final print was the version taken forward for testing on the FESTO CP assembly line.
