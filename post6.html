Building the Virtual Factory: Tecnomatrix

Step by step: how I modelled the FESTO line in Plant Simulation

Tecnomatix: Plant Simulation

Tecnomatix Plant Simulation is a discrete event simulation software developed by Siemens that allows engineers to build virtual models of manufacturing systems and production lines. Rather than physically constructing a factory to test its performance, Plant Simulation enables the entire process to be modelled digitally — machines, conveyors, workers, shift patterns, and buffers can all be represented as objects and connected together to replicate real-world production flow. The simulation can then be run over any time period to measure output, identify bottlenecks, and test different production scenarios before any real investment is made. Negahban and Smith (2014) describe discrete event simulation as one of the most powerful tools available to manufacturing engineers, enabling the analysis of complex production systems that would be impossible to evaluate analytically — making it particularly valuable for a multi-stage process like the Phones-R-Us assembly line where the interaction between 3D printing capacity and assembly line throughput determines overall system output.

Simulation Setup

The initial simulation was built to replicate the FESTO CP assembly line in isolation — before the 3D printing stage was introduced. The model consists of six SingleProc objects representing each assembly station, connected in sequence by conveyors. A Source object feeds parts into the line and a Drain at the end counts completed phones. The ShiftCalendar was configured to run 9:00–17:00, Monday to Friday, with two break periods, over 48 working weeks — representing a standard single-shift factory operation. A WorkerPool object was included to represent the assembly line operators, and a BottleneckAnalyzer was added to automatically identify the slowest station during the simulation run. The Chart object provides a visual Resource Statistics breakdown showing the percentage of time each station spends working, waiting, paused, and blocked. The simulation was set to run for 365 days to generate annual output figures directly comparable to the 50,000 phones/year target.

Station Times for Simulation

Station Name Processing Time (seconds) Notes
1 Front Mag Station 9s Fastest station — never the bottleneck
2 Measuring Station 12s Laser dimensional check
3 Pick By Light Station 22s Operator guided component placement
4 Press Station 11s Machine presses top casing onto bottom
5 Oven Station 29s Bottleneck — slowest station on the line
6 Output Station 9s Completed assembly exits the line

With these cycle times entered and the simulation run over 365 days, the assembly line alone produced approximately 238,000 phones per year — nearly five times the 50,000 target. At first glance this appears to comfortably meet demand. However, this result only reflects the capacity of the assembly line in isolation, with an unlimited supply of parts being fed in. Within the line itself, the Oven Station at 29 seconds is the clear bottleneck — as the slowest station, it dictates the maximum throughput of the entire line regardless of how fast the other five stations operate. The BottleneckAnalyzer confirmed this, highlighting the Oven Station as the limiting factor. The Resource Statistics chart showed that stations upstream of the Oven Station spent a significant proportion of their time in a blocked state — unable to pass their completed part to the next station because the Oven was still processing the previous one. This phenomenon, known as blocking, is a direct consequence of the bottleneck and represents wasted capacity at the faster upstream stations. In the next post, the 3D printing stage is introduced into the simulation — and the true bottleneck of the complete production system shifts dramatically from the Oven Station to the printing process itself.

References

1. Negahban, A. and Smith, J.S. (2014) 'Simulation for manufacturing system design and operation: Literature review and analysis', Journal of Manufacturing Systems, 33(2), pp. 241–261.
