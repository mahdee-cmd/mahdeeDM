FESTO Lab · Week 4–5

Inside the Assembly Line: Observing the FESTO CP Lab

Six stations, cycle times, and live run of prototypes.

The FESTO CP Lab

The FESTO CP Lab is a modular cyber-physical assembly system designed to replicate the conditions of a real industrial production line in an educational setting. It consists of a series of automated and semi-automated stations connected by a conveyor system, through which parts are transported and assembled in sequence. The lab provides a practical environment for students and engineers to observe, measure, and analyse real manufacturing processes without the cost or risk associated with a live factory floor. In the context of this project, the FESTO line represents the assembly stage of the Phones-R-Us production process; the point at which the 3D printed casings produced in the earlier stages of the project are brought together with components from other suppliers and assembled into a finished phone. Festo Didactic (2020) describe the CP lab as a platform for experiencing the full scope of Industry 4.0 technologies in practice, including real-time data acquisition, process monitoring, and cyber-physical integration, all of which are directly relevant to the digital manufacturing themes explored throughout this module.

The Six Stations

The FESTO CP lab assembly line consists of six stations, each performing a specific function in the phone assembly process. The first station takes the bottom case and puts it on a conveyor. The second station performs a measuring and quality check using a laser sensor to verify key dimensions of the casing. The third station uses a pick-by-light system to guide the operator in placing the correct internal components. The fourth station is the press station, where a machine presses the top casing down onto the bottom casing to secure the assembly. The fifth station is the oven station, which applies heat to cure and fix the internal components in place. The sixth and final station is the output station, where the completed assembly exits the line and is counted as a finished unit.

The Bottleneck

Within the FESTO assembly line itself, the Oven Station at 29 seconds is the clear bottleneck: the slowest station in the sequence, dictating the maximum throughput of the entire line. No matter how fast the other five stations operate, the line cannot produce a completed phone any faster than one every 29 seconds. On a standard 8-hour shift over 48 working weeks this equates to a theoretical maximum of approximately 238,000 phones per year from the assembly line alone which is well above the 50,000 target. However, when the full production process was modelled in Tecnomatix Plant Simulation including the 3D printing stage, the true bottleneck of the entire system became immediately apparent: the printers. With a 2-hour cycle time per casing, the 3D printing process was orders of magnitude slower than the assembly line, and 70 printers running in parallel were ultimately required to feed the line at a sufficient rate to meet the 50,000 phones per year target. This shift in bottleneck, from the Oven Station in isolation to the AM Printing stage in the full simulation, is one of the most important insights generated by the plant simulation work and demonstrates the value of modelling the complete end-to-end process rather than individual stages in isolation.
